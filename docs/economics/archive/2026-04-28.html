Top Papers This Week

China's Hidden $2.1 Trillion Global Empire: Far Larger Than Official Records Show

Title: China's Global Ownership | Authors: Jennie Bai, Luc Laeven, Yaojun Ke, Hong Ru

By tracing ownership through complex offshore structures, researchers discovered that Chinese-controlled foreign assets reached $2.1 trillion by 2021—roughly 3% of all global corporate assets and growing at 20% annually. This hidden footprint is substantially larger than what official foreign direct investment statistics reveal, with state-owned enterprises leading the charge. The findings reshape our understanding of China's true economic influence worldwide and highlight how traditional FDI measurements miss the full picture of global capital flows.

AI Job Impact Studies Are Dangerously Unreliable, New Research Warns

Title: How (un)Stable Are LLM Occupational Exposure Scores? Evidence from Multi-Model Replication | Authors: Michelle Yin, Hoa Vu, Claudia Persico

The growing literature on AI's labor market effects relies on large language models to assess which jobs are most at risk—but these measurements are alarmingly unstable. When researchers replicated the same analysis with three different AI models, they found a 3.6-fold difference in average exposure scores and agreement as low as 57%. This fragility is so severe that policy conclusions can flip from statistically significant negative effects to insignificant positive ones depending on which model is used, calling into question a rapidly expanding field of economic research.

The Rise of Fascism: How "Ordinary" Germans Joined the Nazi Party

Title: Hysteresis and Selection in the Rise of Fascism: The 'Ordinary Men' of the Nazi Party | Authors: Luis Bosshart, Max Deter, Leander Heldring, Cathrin C. Mohr, Matthias Weigand

Using digitized records of nearly all Nazi Party members, economists reveal how extremist movements can mainstream themselves over time. As the party expanded, its membership increasingly resembled the general German population in occupation, demographics, and religion—contradicting the notion that only fringe elements supported fascism. The study also uncovers powerful social contagion effects: early Nazi membership within communities, workplaces, and families significantly increased the likelihood that others would join, providing crucial insights into how radical movements achieve mass appeal.

Macroeconomics

This week's macroeconomic research explores foundational questions about human capital development and historical institutional arrangements that continue to shape modern economies.

Early Childcare Attendance and Cognitive Skills in Adolescence

Authors: Ingvild Almås, Nina Drange, Costas Meghir, Henrik D. Zachrisson

Using a natural experiment from Norway's 2003 childcare expansion, this study finds that starting childcare one year earlier boosts math scores by nearly 10% of a standard deviation by grade 9. The effects are particularly striking for disadvantaged children—those with mothers lacking high school diplomas see gains of 28% of a standard deviation, reducing the achievement gap by about one-third. Children of immigrants also experience significant improvements. Why it matters: These findings provide compelling evidence that early childhood investments can reduce inequality and boost long-term human capital formation, with implications for education policy worldwide.

Comparative Advantage and Colonial Monopoly: The Political Economy of Exclusive Trading Companies

Authors: Sebastian Galiani, Ivan Lopez Cruz, Alessandra A. Palazzo, Gustavo Torrens

This paper examines how colonial powers used exclusive trading companies to extract wealth from their colonies, essentially functioning as export taxes that created price wedges between domestic and international markets. The researchers demonstrate that the economic impact of these monopolies depended critically on whether the colony and metropolis were economic competitors or had complementary comparative advantages. When colonies and metropolitan centers competed in similar goods, the extraction mechanism worked differently than when they specialized in complementary products. Why it matters: Understanding these historical institutional arrangements helps explain persistent patterns of economic development and trade relationships that continue to influence global economic structures today.

Key Themes This Week

Both papers, while examining vastly different time periods and contexts, highlight how institutions—whether modern childcare systems or colonial trading arrangements—can either perpetuate or reduce economic inequalities. The childcare study demonstrates how well-designed public policies can break cycles of disadvantage, while the colonial monopoly research reveals how extractive institutions systematically transferred wealth from periphery to center, creating lasting economic disparities.

The methodological approaches also showcase the breadth of modern economic research: the childcare study leverages quasi-experimental variation from policy changes to establish causation, while the colonial trade paper combines theoretical modeling with historical analysis to understand complex institutional dynamics. Together, they illustrate how economic research can inform both contemporary policy debates and our understanding of long-term developmental patterns.

Microeconomics

This week's microeconomics research reveals how market power emerges in unexpected places and challenges conventional wisdom about competition, technology, and worker protection.

Market Power in Mortgage Pricing: the Role of Referral Lending

Authors: Dayin Zhang, Panle Jia Barwick, Lu Han, Jonathan Kroah

Despite fierce competition among mortgage lenders, homebuyers still face significant price differences—and real estate agents may be partly to blame. This study reveals that 85% of realtors funnel over 40% of their clients to fewer than four loan officers, creating concentrated referral networks that limit borrowers' effective choice. Borrowers using these referred loan officers pay 18.6 basis points higher mortgage rates, costing them an average of $2,609 upfront. Why it matters: This hidden source of market power shows how intermediaries can undermine competition even in seemingly competitive markets.

Intermediate Input Prices and the Labor Share

Authors: Juanma Castro-Vincenzi, Benny Kleinman

The declining share of income going to workers has puzzled economists for decades, but this research points to an overlooked culprit: rising materials costs. When the price of intermediate inputs like raw materials increases, it squeezes the labor share of value-added income while boosting profit margins—even without changes in market power or technology. The authors provide both causal evidence across U.S. industries and a quantitative model demonstrating this mechanism's importance. Why it matters: This offers a simpler explanation for labor share trends that doesn't require invoking rising monopoly power or technological displacement.

The Welfare Effects of Protecting Older Workers

Authors: Todd Morris, Stefan Staubli, Benoit Dostie

When five Canadian provinces banned mandatory retirement between 2005-2009, the policy delivered unexpectedly positive results across the board. Post-65 employment and earnings rose by at least 14%, older workers increased their savings, spouses delayed retirement, and public finances improved. Crucially, there were no negative spillovers to younger workers or firm profitability, as companies adjusted through hiring rather than cutting existing workers. Why it matters: This challenges concerns that protecting older workers hurts younger generations or business performance.

How (un)Stable Are LLM Occupational Exposure Scores?

Authors: Michelle Yin, Hoa Vu, Claudia Persico

A growing body of research uses AI models to assess which jobs are most exposed to automation—but this study reveals a troubling problem with this approach. When three different large language models evaluated identical occupational tasks, their assessments diverged dramatically, with only 57% agreement in some cases. This measurement instability completely altered empirical conclusions, with some results flipping from significantly negative to insignificantly positive depending on which model was used. Why it matters: This highlights serious methodological concerns for the rapidly expanding literature on AI's labor market impacts.

Econometrics & Methods

This week showcases econometrics tackling diverse policy challenges, from measuring war's domestic political effects to evaluating digital market interventions and innovative data collection methods.

The Domestic Political Economy of War: Evidence from Russia

Authors: Alena Gorbuntsova, Gaurav Khanna, Sultan Mehmood

Using a difference-in-differences event-study design around two major escalations (2014 Crimea annexation and 2022 invasion), this study reveals how Russia's war against Ukraine served domestic political purposes. Provinces with larger ethnic Russian populations showed sharp increases in support for Putin following both episodes, while battlefield casualties disproportionately fell on regions with fewer ethnic Russians. Why it matters: This provides rigorous econometric evidence of how authoritarian regimes can use external conflicts to consolidate domestic power while strategically distributing the costs of war.

The Effect of Choice Screens on Mobile Browser Usage: Evidence from the EU Digital Markets Act

Authors: Jesper Akesson, Kush Amlani, Raul Cepeda Suarez, Emily Chissell, Stefan Hunt, Michael Luca, Gemma Petrie

Leveraging the EU's Digital Markets Act mandate requiring browser choice screens, researchers found substantial effects using difference-in-differences analysis. Firefox usage increased 113% on iOS and 12% on Android relative to counterfactual scenarios, with the gap explained by different rollout strategies across platforms. Why it matters: This demonstrates that well-designed choice architecture can effectively counter default bias, providing crucial evidence for competition policy in digital markets.

Effects of Expanding Contraceptive Choice: New Evidence from Virginia's Contraceptive Access Initiative

Authors: Jessica H. Kiser, Analisa Packham, Janelle Anthony, Evelyn Escobar, Emily Yeatts

Using encounter-level clinic data and county-level natality data from 2014-2021, this difference-in-differences study found Virginia's 2018 Contraceptive Access Initiative reduced birth rates by approximately 3% in participating counties. The smaller effect compared to similar state programs appears driven by existing high LARC take-up and contraceptive substitution. Why it matters: This highlights how baseline conditions and substitution effects can significantly influence the effectiveness of public health interventions.

A Blended Data Approach to Measuring Monthly Housing Starts: Satellite Imagery, Survey Data and More!

Authors: Nicole Czaplicki, Colin J. Shevlin, Hector R. Ferronato, Aidan D. Smith, Dwarakh V. Nayam, Lei Peng, Scott W. Springer, Doren Walker

The U.S. Census Bureau is pioneering the use of convolutional neural networks applied to monthly satellite imagery to identify housing starts, potentially supplementing traditional field representative interviews. This machine learning approach aims to enhance the Survey of Construction's measurement of new residential construction. Why it matters: This represents a significant methodological advance in official statistics, demonstrating how alternative data sources and AI can improve the timeliness and accuracy of key economic indicators.
