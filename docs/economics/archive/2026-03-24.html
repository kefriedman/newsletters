Top Papers This Week

The American Job Ladder Has Collapsed—And It's Killing Wage Growth

Title: The Long-Term Decline of the U.S. Job Ladder | Authors: Niklas Engbom, Aniket Baksy, Daniele Caratelli

American workers today are about half as likely to receive a better-paying job offer as they were in the 1980s, according to four decades of labor market data. This dramatic weakening of the "job ladder"—workers' ability to climb to better positions—helps explain why wages have stagnated despite low unemployment. The culprits appear to be rising employer concentration and reduced labor market dynamism, fundamentally altering how the American economy distributes opportunity and income.

AI Is Already Boosting Productivity—But Not How You'd Expect

Title: Artificial Intelligence, Productivity, and the Workforce: Evidence from Corporate Executives | Authors: Salomé Baslandze, Zachary Edwards, John Graham, et al.

A survey of nearly 750 corporate executives reveals that AI is delivering real productivity gains across sectors, with the largest effects in high-skill services and finance. Surprisingly, these gains aren't coming from replacing workers with machines, but from increasing revenue per employee—suggesting AI is augmenting human capabilities rather than simply substituting for them. The findings provide the first comprehensive executive-level evidence of AI's economic impact as it unfolds in real-time.

California's $20 Fast Food Minimum Wage Raised Prices Exactly as Much as Economists Predicted

Title: The Effects of California's $20 Fast Food Minimum Wage on Prices | Authors: Jeffrey Clemens, Olivia Edwards, Jonathan Meer, Joshua D. Nguyen

California's groundbreaking $20 minimum wage for fast food workers—a 25% increase—led to food prices rising by 3.3-3.6% compared to other states, according to Bureau of Labor Statistics data. The study provides real-time evidence of how dramatic minimum wage increases flow through to consumers, offering crucial insights as other states and cities consider similar policies. The price effects were precisely contained to food away from home, with no spillovers to other goods and services.

Macroeconomics

This week brings fresh perspectives on central bank policy tools and international economic dynamics, with researchers exploring how monetary authorities can better manage financial volatility and how global climate commitments intersect with economic development.

Financial Conditions Targeting in a Multi-Asset Open Economy

Authors: Ricardo J. Caballero, Alp Simsek

Central banks may achieve better macroeconomic outcomes by targeting a "financial conditions index" that combines exchange rates and domestic asset prices, rather than responding to each market separately. The research shows that when noise traders and limited arbitrage capacity create volatility across financial markets, monetary policy actions in one market inevitably spill over into others. By committing to stronger, more systematic responses to a weighted average of financial conditions, central banks can improve risk diversification and deliver superior macroeconomic stability. Why it matters: This provides a concrete framework for central banks grappling with how to respond when multiple financial markets are experiencing stress simultaneously.

Climate Fairness and Growth: Allocating the Remaining Carbon Budget

Authors: Galina Hale, Michael Halling, Nora Alice Paulus, Han H.G. Pham

Meeting the 1.5°C climate target requires dividing up a finite remaining carbon budget among 162 countries, but "fair" allocations based on equal cumulative per-capita emissions may conflict with economic development needs. Using historical data from 1950-2023, the researchers find that equity-based carbon allocations could severely constrain growth prospects for emerging and developing economies, as these countries typically need to increase emissions as incomes rise. The study reveals a fundamental tension between climate equity principles and sustained economic development in lower-income nations. Why it matters: This quantifies a key challenge in international climate negotiations and highlights the need for innovative solutions that balance fairness with development imperatives.

An 'Austrian' Model of International Specialization

Authors: Pol Antràs, Adrian Kulesza

Countries with more patient consumers and better-developed financial markets tend to specialize in production processes that take longer to complete, according to this novel trade model inspired by Austrian capital theory. The key insight is that longer production processes require more financing over time, creating higher costs in countries with elevated interest rates. Nations with lower financing costs therefore gain comparative advantage in sectors requiring extended production periods, while higher-cost countries focus on quicker-turnaround activities. Why it matters: This provides a new lens for understanding how financial development shapes international trade patterns and suggests that improving capital markets can shift a country's export specialization toward more complex, longer-term production.

Microeconomics

This week brings fascinating insights into how human capital, management, and market structures shape economic outcomes, from the boardroom to the job market to pharmaceutical innovation.

Making The Invisible Hand Visible: Managers and The Allocation of Workers to Jobs

Authors: Virginia Minni

Using personnel records from 200,000 workers across 100 countries, this study reveals that good managers don't just supervise—they actively reallocate workers to better-matched positions within firms through strategic transfers. The research shows these managerial decisions generate large, persistent gains in worker productivity and career progression, essentially making Adam Smith's "invisible hand" visible through concrete management actions. Why it matters: This provides hard evidence that management quality drives firm performance primarily through optimizing worker-job matches rather than just traditional oversight.

The Long-Term Decline of the U.S. Job Ladder

Authors: Niklas Engbom, Aniket Baksy, Daniele Caratelli

American workers today are roughly half as likely to receive better-paying job offers compared to the 1980s, contributing significantly to four decades of wage stagnation. The authors trace this decline to rising employer concentration and increased use of noncompete agreements that limit job shopping opportunities. Their model suggests these structural changes have reduced annual real wage growth substantially across the economy. Why it matters: This explains a key mechanism behind growing inequality and provides policy-relevant evidence about how market concentration affects worker mobility and wages.

What Makes New Work Different from More Work?

Authors: David Autor, Caroline Chin, Anna M. Salomons, Bryan Seegmiller

Novel occupational roles that emerge with technological change systematically attract younger, more educated workers who earn significant wage premiums that persist over time. These premiums reflect returns to scarce, specialized expertise rather than temporary market imbalances, suggesting that "new work" creates genuine value through innovation. The research spans from 1940 to 2023, providing a comprehensive view of how labor markets adapt to technological disruption. Why it matters: This offers an optimistic counternarrative to automation fears, showing how technological change creates high-value work opportunities for those who can develop relevant expertise.

From Free Rider to Innovator: The Rise of China's Drug Development

Authors: Panle Jia Barwick, Hongyuan Xia, Tianli Xia

China transformed from conducting less than 8% of global clinical trials in 2010 to surpassing the US in annual trial volume by 2020, driven primarily by healthcare reimbursement reforms that expanded market size for innovative drugs. This rapid transition from pharmaceutical "free rider" to global innovator demonstrates how policy changes can dramatically alter innovation incentives in large markets. Why it matters: This case study illustrates how developing countries can transition from imitation to innovation through strategic market-expanding policies.

Econometrics & Methods

This week in econometrics brings a methodological breakthrough that bridges the gap between purely empirical and fully structural approaches to economic estimation.

Reserve Demand Estimation with Minimal Theory

Authors: Ricardo Lagos, Gastón Navarro

Lagos and Navarro introduce an innovative "middle ground" approach to estimating reserve demand that combines the simplicity of reduced-form econometric methods with essential theoretical constraints. Their method incorporates minimal theoretical restrictions while controlling for administered-rate spread changes that cause shifts in reserve demand, yet remains as user-friendly as traditional atheoretical approaches. When applied to U.S. data, their estimates align well with more complex structural models, suggesting this hybrid methodology captures the essential dynamics without excessive computational burden. Why it matters: This approach could become a new standard for monetary economics research, offering researchers a way to maintain theoretical rigor without sacrificing empirical tractability.

Methodological Spotlight: This paper exemplifies a growing trend in econometrics toward "theory-informed empirics" – methods that respect economic theory's core insights while avoiding the full complexity of structural estimation. This balanced approach is particularly valuable for policy-relevant research where both theoretical coherence and empirical flexibility are essential.

Looking Ahead

With only one major paper this week, the field appears to be in a consolidation phase, likely preparing for the upcoming conference season. The Lagos-Navarro methodology represents exactly the kind of innovation that advances the field – not by completely revolutionizing existing approaches, but by thoughtfully combining their strengths while mitigating their weaknesses.

Their reserve demand estimation strategy is particularly timely given ongoing debates about monetary policy implementation and the role of bank reserves in financial markets. As central banks worldwide grapple with unconventional monetary policies and their transmission mechanisms, having robust yet practical tools for estimating reserve demand becomes increasingly important.

The paper also highlights an important trend in modern econometrics: the recognition that the traditional dichotomy between "reduced-form" and "structural" methods may be unnecessarily limiting. By showing that minimal theoretical restrictions can substantially improve empirical estimates without significantly complicating the methodology, Lagos and Navarro point toward a more nuanced approach to empirical research design.
