Top Papers This Week

Tariffs Are Economic Poison: 180 Years of Data Prove Protectionism Backfires

Title: The Macroeconomic Effects of Tariffs: Insights from 180 Years of U.S. Trade Policy | Authors: Tamar den Besten, Regis Barnichon, Diego R. Känzig, Aayush Singh

Using nearly two centuries of U.S. trade data, this landmark study definitively shows that tariff increases are contractionary—they reduce imports, exports, output, and manufacturing activity while raising prices. The research comes at a critical moment as policymakers debate new tariff policies, providing the most comprehensive historical evidence yet that protectionist measures consistently harm economic growth. The findings reveal that tariffs transmit damage through both supply and demand channels, making this essential reading for understanding trade policy's true costs.

Work-From-Home Revolution Permanently Rewired Innovation: Patent Data Shows Lasting Tech Shift

Title: Demand-Driven Technical Change: Evidence from WFH Technologies | Authors: Steven J. Davis, Nicholas Bloom, Mihai A. Codreanu

COVID-19 didn't just change where we work—it permanently altered what inventors create. Analyzing 5.6 million patent applications through 2026, researchers found that work-from-home technology patents surged by two-thirds during the pandemic and remain 50% above pre-pandemic levels five years later. This represents the first real-time documentation of how sudden demand shocks can permanently redirect the path of technological progress, with implications for productivity, urban planning, and the future of work itself.

All-Female Medical Teams Save More Lives: Massive Study Reveals Gender's Role in Healthcare Outcomes

Title: Gender Mix and Team Performance: Evidence from Obstetrics | Authors: Ambar La Forgia, Manasvini Singh

In a groundbreaking analysis of 2.5 million births, researchers discovered that all-female obstetric teams achieve the best maternal outcomes while all-male teams produce the worst results—with severe complications 15.8% higher under male-only teams. Even more intriguingly, mixed-gender teams perform better when led by men rather than women, suggesting complex dynamics in how gender composition affects high-stakes medical decision-making. These findings have profound implications for hospital staffing decisions and our understanding of team performance in critical settings.

Macroeconomics

This week brings fascinating insights into how trade wars reshape inflation expectations and the complex interplay between economic integration and geopolitical conflict.

Tariffs and the Term Structure of Inflation Expectations

Authors: Stéphane Auray, Michael B. Devereux, Anthony M. Diercks, Aurélien Eyquem, Joon Kim

Financial markets showed unprecedented behavior in 2025 when tariff announcements caused short-term and long-term inflation expectations to move in opposite directions for the first time on record. The researchers demonstrate that standard economic models, which predict tariffs simply raise price levels once, cannot explain this new pattern where markets expect different inflation impacts across time horizons. Why it matters: This suggests tariff policies may have more complex and unpredictable effects on monetary policy and market expectations than previously understood.

Bilateral Conflict Risk and Trade: Military Wars, Trade Wars, and Diplomatic Noise

Authors: Joshua Aizenman, Rodolphe Desbordes, Jamel Saadaoui

By analyzing global conflict data, researchers found that governments have increasingly shifted from military confrontation to economic coercion over the past decade, nearly doubling the share of hostility channeled through trade-related tensions. Their new measurement system distinguishes between different types of conflict—from missile strikes to diplomatic protests—revealing that "trade wars" have become the preferred tool of international pressure. Why it matters: This shift toward economic warfare suggests traditional models of international trade may need to account for rising non-military but economically disruptive conflicts.

The "Peace Dividend" of International Trade: A New Empirical Approach

Authors: Ling Feng, Qiuyue Huang, Zhiyuan Li, Christopher M. Meissner

Using innovative methods that exploit changes in air versus sea transport technology, this study provides strong evidence that increased trade between countries significantly reduces both the likelihood and intensity of military conflicts. The research addresses longstanding questions about whether trade truly promotes peace by using exogenous variation in trade patterns from 1962 to 2014. Why it matters: These findings support the case for maintaining international economic integration as a tool for global stability, particularly relevant amid current geopolitical tensions and deglobalization trends.

Treasury Supply Shocks: Propagation Through Debt Expansion and Maturity Adjustment

Authors: Huixin Bi, Maxime Phillot, Sarah Zubairy

With U.S. debt at historically high levels, this research separates two distinct effects of Treasury market activity: increases in total debt volume raise borrowing costs across all maturities and crowd out private investment, while shifts toward longer-term debt steepen the yield curve but have different economic impacts. The study uses high-frequency data around Treasury auction announcements to identify these causal effects. Why it matters: As fiscal deficits remain elevated, understanding how different aspects of government debt issuance affect financial markets and private sector activity becomes crucial for policy design.

Microeconomics

This week's microeconomics research spans fascinating organizational puzzles—from why universities have thrived for nearly a millennium to how gender dynamics shape team performance in high-stakes medical settings.

How Have Universities Survived for Nearly a Millenium

Authors: David M. Cutler, Edward L. Glaeser

Universities have evolved from medieval teachers' guilds into complex, multi-product organizations by maintaining a unique structure that grants faculty remarkable autonomy. This intellectual freedom attracts knowledge-seeking individuals and enables universities to adapt and morph in ways that traditional firms cannot, though it also explains their frequent internal conflicts and tensions with authorities. Why it matters: Understanding this organizational model offers insights into how institutions can maintain longevity while continuously innovating across diverse fields.

Gender Mix and Team Performance: Evidence from Obstetrics

Authors: Ambar La Forgia, Manasvini Singh

Analyzing 2.5 million births, researchers found that all-female obstetrician teams achieve the best maternal outcomes while all-male teams perform worst, with severe maternal complications 15.8% higher in male-only teams. Surprisingly, female-led mixed-gender teams underperformed compared to male-led mixed teams, suggesting complex team dynamics beyond individual physician skill levels. Why it matters: This challenges conventional wisdom about gender diversity and highlights how team composition directly affects performance in critical, high-stakes environments.

Impact Trickles Down: A General Equilibrium Theory of Stakeholder Exit and Engagement

Authors: Briana Chang, Harrison Hong

Purpose-driven stakeholders choose different strategies—exit versus engagement—depending on whether social harm scales with a firm's productivity. When harm correlates with production levels, high-productivity firms can outbid others for profit-focused stakeholders, forcing purpose-driven stakeholders to exit, which creates cascading effects throughout the productivity hierarchy. Why it matters: This framework explains why studies may underestimate the aggregate impact of seemingly ineffective stakeholder activism.

If You Build It, They May Not Come: Willingness to Participate in Managed EV Charging

Authors: Fiona Burlig, James B. Bushnell, David S. Rapson

Despite promising results from volunteer-based studies, a population-wide experiment of managed EV charging programs revealed critically low enrollment rates even with high incentives, leading to negligible effects on electricity consumption. This stark contrast with earlier research highlights how self-selection bias can dramatically overestimate program effectiveness. Why it matters: The findings underscore the importance of population-wide testing before scaling energy policies that rely on voluntary participation.

Belief Dispersion and Entrepreneurial Entry

Authors: Joshua S. Gans

Entrepreneurs enter markets based not just on their own conviction about opportunities, but on their assessment of how optimistic rivals might be, creating a complex relationship between belief dispersion and market entry that can result in peak entry occurring at intermediate levels of uncertainty rather than at extremes.

Econometrics & Methods

This week showcases the versatility of econometric methods, from leveraging natural experiments in development economics to modeling complex strategic interactions and uncovering market inefficiencies through textual analysis.

The Impact of Maternal Education on Early Childhood Development

Authors: Moriam Khanam, Mohammad Hajizadeh, Casey Warman

This study provides compelling evidence for the causal impact of maternal education using a clever natural experiment from Bangladesh's female stipend program. The researchers use instrumental variables to overcome the classic endogeneity problem—more educated mothers might differ in unobservable ways that also affect child development. They find that each additional year of maternal schooling boosts children's developmental scores by 0.5 points on a 10-point scale, with particularly strong effects on literacy and numeracy skills. Why it matters: This rigorous identification strategy provides policymakers with credible evidence that investing in girls' education generates intergenerational benefits, making a strong case for educational interventions in developing countries.

Le Bureau des Légendes: A Dynamic Theory of Double Agents

Authors: Sebastian Galiani, Franco Mettola La Giglia

This paper tackles an unconventional but fascinating application of game theory and dynamic modeling to intelligence operations. The authors model double agents as players in a trilateral game, deriving closed-form solutions for what they call the "corridor of survival"—the conditions under which a double agent can maintain their precarious position. Their linear-quadratic-Gaussian framework yields analytical results showing that double-agent relationships are inherently unstable and doomed to eventual collapse due to belief updating and compounding risks. Why it matters: Beyond its intriguing subject matter, this work demonstrates how sophisticated econometric modeling can illuminate strategic behavior in high-stakes, information-asymmetric environments with applications to contract theory and mechanism design.

The Inefficient Pricing of News

Authors: Antoine Didisheim, Bryan T. Kelly, Mohammad Pourmohammadi, Hanqing Tian

This research addresses a fundamental challenge in behavioral finance: separating genuine market inefficiency from predictable patterns in information flow. The authors develop an innovative methodology to extract "news shocks" by purging news text of its predictable components based on stock characteristics, essentially creating a cleaner measure of unexpected information. These purified news shocks predict returns for up to 18 months and exhibit larger effect sizes than virtually all known market anomalies. The study reveals systematic patterns in how investors process different types of news—underreacting to negative and quantitative information while overreacting to high-attention and ambiguous content. Why it matters: This work advances our understanding of market efficiency by providing a more rigorous framework for testing how markets process textual information, with significant implications for both academic finance and practical trading strategies.

These papers collectively demonstrate the breadth of modern econometric applications, from development policy evaluation using natural experiments to sophisticated modeling of strategic behavior and innovative approaches to testing market efficiency through textual analysis.
