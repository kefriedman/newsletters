AI Is Creating a Dangerous New Digital Divide Between Europe and America

Title: Mind the Gap: AI Adoption in Europe and the U.S. | Authors: Alexander Bick, Adam Blandin, David J. Deming, Nicola Fuchs-Schündeln, Jonas Jessen

Using cutting-edge 2025-2026 survey data, researchers document alarming gaps in AI adoption between the US and Europe that could reshape global economic competitiveness. The study reveals that differences in firm management practices and whether companies actively encourage AI use are key drivers of adoption rates. With AI generating meaningful time savings for workers, these adoption gaps could translate into significant productivity advantages for early-adopting regions, potentially altering the balance of economic power between continents.

Brazil's Cash Transfer Program Saved 1,000 Lives While Actually Increasing Employment

Title: Cash Transfers and Productive Inclusion: Evidence from Bolsa Familia | Authors: Michael C. Best, Felipe Lobel, Valdemar Pinho Neto

This groundbreaking study of the world's largest cash transfer program demolishes the conventional wisdom that welfare reduces work incentives. When Brazil expanded its Bolsa Família program, employment rose 5% while hospitalization fell 8% and mortality dropped 14%, saving roughly 1,000 lives. The findings suggest that cash transfers can actually expand labor supply by relieving health and subsistence constraints that prevent people from working productively—a paradigm shift with profound implications for social policy worldwide.

The Federal Reserve Has Been Fighting Different Types of Inflation Differently for Decades

Title: In the Fed's Mind | Authors: Ali Kakhbod, Amir Kermani, Bernardo Maciel

By systematically analyzing 88 years of Federal Reserve meeting records, researchers reveal that the Fed has secretly been playing favorites with inflation since the Volcker era. The central bank responds much more aggressively to demand-driven price increases than to supply-driven ones—and financial markets have caught on to this asymmetric approach. This discovery helps explain why supply shocks have more persistent effects on realized inflation and could fundamentally change how we understand monetary policy transmission and market expectations.

Macroeconomics

This week brings fresh experimental evidence on how fiscal policy expectations drive household behavior, plus important insights on universities' broader economic impact.

The Effects of Fiscal News on Household Expectations and Spending: New Causal Evidence

Authors: Myungkyu Shim, Kwang Hwan Kim, Myunghwan Andrew Lee, Sangyup Choi, Siye Bae, Olivier Coibion, Yuriy Gorodnichenko

Using an innovative experiment with 11,000 Korean households, researchers provided different groups with varying information about government debt and deficits, then tracked their actual spending through linked transaction data. They found that when people expect higher future government debt, they anticipate more inflation and increase their current spending—likely trying to buy goods before prices rise. Conversely, expectations of improved fiscal balance (lower deficits) boost confidence in economic growth but don't significantly change spending patterns. Why it matters: This provides rare causal evidence of how fiscal policy communications directly influence consumer behavior, offering crucial insights for policymakers crafting budget announcements.

Real Effects of Academic Research Revisited

Authors: Adam B. Jaffe, Laura B. Shupp, Valentina Tartari

This comprehensive survey examines decades of research on how universities contribute to economic growth through three key channels: creating new knowledge, educating students, and facilitating technology transfer and entrepreneurship. The authors highlight how recent advances in linking data across inventors, institutions, and innovations—combined with network analysis methods—have strengthened evidence of universities' causal economic impact. The effects vary significantly across academic disciplines and economic sectors, with some fields showing stronger spillovers to local economies. Why it matters: As governments worldwide debate higher education funding and research priorities, this synthesis provides essential evidence on universities' measurable contributions to innovation and growth.

Microeconomics

This week's microeconomics research tackles some of society's biggest questions, from how AI is reshaping work and regulation to whether cash transfers actually discourage employment (spoiler: they don't).

Cash Transfers and Productive Inclusion: Evidence from Bolsa Familia

Authors: Michael C. Best, Felipe Lobel, Valdemar Pinho Neto

Challenging conventional wisdom, this study of Brazil's massive cash transfer program finds that increasing benefits to the extremely poor actually raised employment by 5 percent while dramatically improving health outcomes—hospitalization fell 8 percent and mortality dropped 14 percent, saving roughly 1,000 lives. Rather than creating dependency, the transfers appear to relax binding subsistence and health constraints that were preventing people from working productively. Why it matters: This evidence supports "productive inclusion" models of welfare policy, suggesting well-designed cash transfers can simultaneously reduce poverty and increase economic participation.

Mind the Gap: AI Adoption in Europe and the U.S.

Authors: Alexander Bick, Adam Blandin, David J. Deming, Nicola Fuchs-Schündeln, Jonas Jessen

Using surveys from 2025-2026, researchers document substantial gaps in AI adoption between the US and Europe, with differences largely explained by worker demographics, firm composition, and management practices that encourage AI use. The study finds AI generates meaningful time savings for workers, and industries with higher AI adoption rates show faster productivity growth. Why it matters: As AI becomes central to economic competitiveness, understanding adoption patterns and their productivity effects is crucial for policy makers concerned about falling behind.

Salience and (Non-)Buyer's Remorse: Optimal Nonlinear Pricing with Cognitively Constrained Consumers

Authors: Aaron L. Bodoh-Creed, Brent R. Hickman, John A. List, Ian Muir, Gregory K. Sun

Using large-scale experiments with Lyft subscriptions, researchers find systematic deviations from the theoretical prediction that consumers will choose pricing plans that minimize their costs. Many high-demand users decline subscriptions that would save them money, while some subscribers fail to break even, suggesting consumers exhibit salience failures and forecasting errors about their future demand. Why it matters: These findings challenge standard pricing theory and suggest firms may be able to exploit consumer cognitive limitations, with implications for both business strategy and consumer protection policy.

Bank Fees and Household Financial Well-Being

Authors: Michaela Pagel, Sharada Sridhar, Emily Williams

Examining recent policy changes by major US banks that eliminated overdraft and NSF fees, researchers find these reforms helped wealthy households more than poor ones. While NSF fee elimination benefited everyone, relaxed overdraft policies only reduced fees for wealthier households, who also saw subsequent declines in late fees and use of alternative financial services like payday loans. Why it matters: The results suggest that modest banking reforms may not be sufficient to meaningfully reduce financial stress for the most vulnerable households, potentially requiring more targeted interventions.

Econometrics & Methods

This week in econometrics brings compelling evidence on education policy effectiveness, with researchers employing sophisticated causal identification strategies to tackle persistent questions about special education and remote learning impacts.

Special Education Substantially Improves Learning: Evidence from Three States

Authors: Stephanie G. Coffey, Joshua Goodman, Amy Ellen Schwartz, Leanna Stiefel, Marcus A. Winters, Yunee H. Yoon

Using an elegant event-study design across Massachusetts, Indiana, and Connecticut, this paper provides the most rigorous evidence to date that special education services genuinely boost student achievement. The researchers cleverly exploit the timing of initial special education classification to identify causal effects, revealing a striking V-shaped pattern where student scores decline before placement but then rise sharply afterward. Within three years, students receiving individualized supports show achievement gains of 0.2-0.4 standard deviations above what they would have achieved otherwise. Why it matters: This settles a long-standing debate about whether special education actually helps students learn or merely provides accommodations, with clear implications for the $100+ billion spent annually on these services.

The Impact of Virtual Instruction on the Transition to College: Evidence from COVID-19

Authors: Nolan G. Pope, Yu Hung Yaow

This paper leverages the natural experiment created by COVID-19 school closures to examine how virtual instruction affected critical college preparation activities. Using a difference-in-differences approach that compares schools with varying degrees of virtual instruction during 2020-2021, the authors find substantial negative effects: fully virtual schools saw FAFSA submissions drop by 4.2 percentage points, ACT participation fall by 4.8 percentage points, and first-year college enrollment decline by 2.5 percentage points. Importantly, while FAFSA submissions partially recovered after schools reopened, ACT participation and college enrollment showed no rebound, suggesting persistent effects. The impacts were dramatically larger at disadvantaged schools, highlighting how the pandemic may have exacerbated educational inequalities. Why it matters: These findings provide crucial evidence for policymakers considering the long-term costs of remote learning and the need for targeted interventions to help students navigate college transitions.

Both papers showcase the power of quasi-experimental methods in education research. The special education study demonstrates how event-study designs can overcome selection bias concerns that have plagued previous research, while the COVID-19 analysis shows how researchers can extract valuable policy insights from unexpected natural experiments. The contrasting findings—one showing positive effects of targeted educational interventions, the other revealing negative consequences of reduced in-person support—underscore the complexity of educational production functions and the importance of rigorous causal identification in informing policy decisions.

Together, these studies contribute to our understanding of how different forms of educational support and delivery methods affect student outcomes, providing evidence that will likely influence special education policy and pandemic recovery efforts for years to come.
