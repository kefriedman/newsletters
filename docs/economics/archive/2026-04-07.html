Top Papers This Week

AI Is Creating an Echo Chamber That Could Break Human Learning

Title: How AI Aggregation Affects Knowledge | Authors: Daron Acemoglu, Tianyi Lin, Asuman Ozdaglar, James Siderius

When AI systems train on human beliefs and feed synthesized information back to society, they can create a dangerous feedback loop that undermines our collective ability to learn truth. MIT economists show that if AI updates too quickly on population beliefs, it can lock society into false consensus, preventing us from reaching accurate conclusions even with infinite data. This isn't just theoretical—it has profound implications for how AI shapes public opinion, scientific consensus, and democratic decision-making in our increasingly AI-mediated information environment.

Doctors Make Life-or-Death Mistakes When They're Cognitively Overloaded

Title: Thinking versus Doing: Cognitive Capacity, Decision Making and Medical Diagnosis | Authors: Benjamin R. Handel, Louis-Jonas Heizlsperger, Jonas Knecht, Jonathan T. Kolstad, Ulrike Malmendier, Filip Matějka

Using granular data from emergency departments, researchers found that when physicians face higher cognitive load, their diagnostic accuracy plummets and they rely more heavily on mental shortcuts. The study reveals how situational stress affects critical decision-making in high-stakes environments, with direct implications for patient outcomes. This research bridges behavioral economics and healthcare policy, suggesting that hospital staffing and workflow design aren't just operational concerns—they're matters of life and death.

Global Warming Is Driving Massive Deforestation as Farmers Desperately Seek New Land

Title: Climate Change, Deforestation, and the Expansion of the Global Agricultural Frontier | Authors: Allan Hsiao, Jacob Moscona, Benjamin A. Olken, Karthik A. Sastry

Climate change isn't just reducing crop yields—it's driving farmers to clear forests at an alarming rate to compensate for lost productivity. Using satellite data from 2001-2019, economists found that extreme heat shocks cause large and persistent forest loss, especially in tropical regions growing temperature-sensitive crops. The effect is strongest where agricultural demand is most inelastic, creating a vicious cycle where climate change accelerates deforestation, which in turn worsens global warming. This research quantifies a critical but underappreciated channel through which climate change destroys natural ecosystems.

Macroeconomics

This week brings fascinating insights on how global connections—from migration to AI networks—reshape economic outcomes, while new research reveals the surprising real-world impacts of seemingly "nominal" financial variables.

Abundance from Abroad: Migrant Income and Long-Run Economic Development

Authors: Gaurav Khanna, Emir Murathanoglu, Caroline Theoharides, Dean Yang

Using the 1997 Asian Financial Crisis as a natural experiment, this study shows that when overseas Filipino workers experienced income shocks, the effects multiplied six-fold over time back home. Remarkably, nearly 75% of long-run income gains came from domestic economic growth rather than remittances themselves, driven partly by increased educational investments. Why it matters: This challenges the view that migration simply drains origin countries, showing instead how it can catalyze broad-based domestic development.

Real Effects of Nominal Interest Rates

Authors: Joshua K. Hausman, John V. Leahy, John Mondragon, Johannes Wieland

Despite economic theory suggesting only real rates matter, this paper demonstrates that nominal interest rates have genuine economic effects through mortgage markets. When nominal rates rise, borrowers face higher constant monthly payments, forcing them to borrow less even if real rates stay constant—a phenomenon most pronounced in cities where payment-to-income constraints bind tightest. Why it matters: This finding has major implications for how we understand monetary policy transmission and housing market dynamics.

How AI Aggregation Affects Knowledge

Authors: Daron Acemoglu, Tianyi Lin, Asuman Ozdaglar, James Siderius

As AI systems increasingly aggregate human knowledge and feed it back to society, this research reveals a critical threshold effect: AI that updates too quickly can actually harm collective learning, while slower-updating systems can improve it. The authors extend classic social learning models to show how AI aggregation creates feedback loops that can either enhance or degrade the quality of information in society. Why it matters: This work provides crucial guidance for designing AI systems that enhance rather than distort our collective intelligence.

"Compensate the Losers?" Economic Policy and the Origins of U.S. Partisan Realignment

Authors: Ilyana Kuziemko, Nicolas Longuet-Marx, Suresh Naidu

This paper offers a fresh explanation for why educated Americans increasingly vote Democratic: it's not about redistribution, but about "predistribution"—policies like minimum wages, unions, and job guarantees that shape market outcomes before taxes and transfers. Educated voters have consistently opposed such policies since the 1940s, and as Democrats moved away from predistribution starting in the 1970s, educated voters became more comfortable with the party. Why it matters: This reframes our understanding of political realignment as driven by changing party positions on market intervention rather than just cultural issues.

Games on Multiplex Networks by Yves Zenou and Junjie Zhou develops new tools for understanding how people allocate effort across different social and economic networks simultaneously, showing why simple network models often miss crucial interactions that shape policy effectiveness.

Microeconomics

This week brings fascinating insights into market power, behavioral economics, and how constraints shape decision-making across diverse settings from financial markets to healthcare.

Market Power and Capital Constraints

Authors: Milena Wittwer, Jason Allen

This study reveals how traders' capital constraints interact with market power to influence asset prices and market liquidity. The researchers find that when capital constraints loosen, asset prices rise but market liquidity actually decreases due to higher price impact. Using Canadian Treasury auction data and a temporary policy exemption during 2020-2021, they show that while these constraints rarely bind, their effects can be substantial when activated. Why it matters: This challenges conventional wisdom about the relationship between capital availability and market functioning, with important implications for financial regulation.

Making Bricks from Straw: Resources and Productivity in Health Care

Authors: Edward N. Okeke

A randomized experiment giving Nigerian public health clinics unexpected grants of ₦600,000 led to remarkable productivity gains when health workers had complete discretion over spending. The funds increased investments in physical and human capital, reduced patient costs, and motivated health workers to perform better. The study provides rare experimental evidence on how financial constraints limit healthcare delivery in developing countries. Why it matters: This demonstrates that resource constraints, not just poor incentives, are a major barrier to effective healthcare in low-income settings.

Learning How To Borrow in a Fintech World: Consumer Behavior When Search Costs Are (Near) Zero

Authors: Alex Günsberg, Camelia M. Kuhnen

Using data from 730,000 loan applications and 200,000 individuals, this research examines how consumers navigate online lending platforms where search costs are virtually zero. Despite the ease of comparison shopping, the authors find substantial variation in loan terms offered to the same applicant, making search highly valuable. However, dynamic adverse selection creates a trade-off: lenders penalize repeat applicants, constraining the benefits of continued searching. Why it matters: As fintech reshapes consumer lending, understanding these search dynamics is crucial for both consumer protection and market efficiency.

Thinking versus Doing: Cognitive Capacity, Decision Making and Medical Diagnosis

Authors: Benjamin R. Handel, Louis-Jonas Heizlsperger, Jonas Knecht, Jonathan T. Kolstad, Ulrike Malmendier, Filip Matějka

This groundbreaking study uses electronic medical records to show how cognitive load affects physician decision-making in emergency departments. When under higher cognitive pressure, doctors substitute mental deliberation with more numerous but less precise diagnostic actions, ordering more tests but with reduced accuracy. The research provides rare real-world evidence of how cognitive constraints affect high-stakes professional decisions. Why it matters: Understanding these behavioral patterns could inform hospital staffing decisions and diagnostic protocols to improve patient outcomes.

Econometrics & Methods

This week brings significant advances in econometric methodology, from improving instrumental variable validation to harnessing machine learning for complex choice models.

Negative Control Falsification Tests for Instrumental Variable Designs

Authors: Oren Danieli, Daniel Nevo, Itai Walk, Bar Weinstein

This American Economic Review paper provides crucial guidance for one of econometrics' most challenging problems: validating instrumental variable designs. The authors show that conventional falsification tests—which use "negative control" variables as proxies for unobserved confounders—can incorrectly flag valid IV designs as problematic. They demonstrate that these tests examine not just the standard independence and exclusion restrictions, but also functional form assumptions that researchers often overlook. Why it matters: This work could prevent researchers from abandoning valid identification strategies and provides clearer implementation guidance for IV validation.

Subjective Earnings and Employment Dynamics

Authors: Manuel Arellano, Orazio Attanasio, Margherita Borella, Mariacristina De Nardi, Gonzalo Paz-Pardo

The authors develop an innovative approach to studying labor market dynamics by using subjective expectations data from the NY Fed Survey of Consumer Expectations. Rather than relying on realized outcomes, they use workers' beliefs about future earnings offers and job acceptance probabilities, which provides direct information about counterfactual scenarios. Their two-step estimation procedure combines fixed-effects regressions with GMM to recover the structure of unobserved heterogeneity in ability, mobility, and match quality. Why it matters: This methodology sidesteps traditional selection bias issues and reveals that individual productivity risk and persistence may be lower than previously estimated.

Amortized Inference for Correlated Discrete Choice Models via Equivariant Neural Networks

Authors: Easton K. Huch, Michael P. Keane

This paper tackles a long-standing limitation in discrete choice modeling: the restrictive assumptions of logit-based models that constrain realistic substitution patterns. The researchers propose using specialized neural networks to approximate choice probabilities for general error distributions, including those with correlated errors, through an "amortized inference" approach. Their method includes custom network architectures designed specifically for choice modeling applications. Why it matters: This could dramatically expand researchers' ability to capture complex, theoretically grounded choice behavior beyond the constraints of traditional logit models.

Investment-based Costs of Equity

Authors: Yicheng Liu, Chen Xue, Lu Zhang

The authors evaluate different approaches to estimating firms' costs of equity capital, comparing their "q5-characteristics model" against traditional accounting-based measures and factor models. Using out-of-sample forecasting methodology, they find their investment-based approach outperforms accounting measures in predicting cross-sectional returns and provides more precise industry-level estimates. Interestingly, they also test machine learning methods (gradient-boosted trees) but find mixed results compared to traditional cross-sectional regressions. Why it matters: Accurate cost of equity estimation is fundamental for corporate finance decisions and asset pricing, making these methodological comparisons valuable for both researchers and practitioners.
