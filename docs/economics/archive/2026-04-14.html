Top Papers This Week

AI Algorithms Are Helping Companies Collude to Charge You More Than Monopolies

Title: Collusion with Optimal Information Disclosure | Authors: Takuo Sugaya, Alexander Wolitzky

This groundbreaking study reveals how third-party pricing algorithms can enable companies to achieve "supra-monopoly prices"—charging consumers even more than a single monopolist would. The research shows that when algorithms selectively share market information between competing firms, they can sustain collusion that leads to rigid pricing and inflated costs. As algorithmic pricing becomes ubiquitous across industries from airlines to retail, this paper provides crucial insights into a potentially massive consumer welfare threat hiding in plain sight.

U.S. Tariffs Hit 80-Year High in 2025, Costing Economy $46-185 Billion

Title: Tariffs in 2025: Short-Run Impacts on the U.S. Economy | Authors: Pablo D. Fajgelbaum, Amit Khandelwal

American tariff rates quadrupled to 9.6% in 2025—the highest level since the 1940s—with 90% of these costs passed directly to U.S. importers and consumers. This timely analysis finds that the new tariffs will reduce U.S. welfare by $46-185 billion annually, far exceeding any potential benefits from improved terms of trade. The research provides real-time evidence of how the return to protectionism is reshaping the American economy, with implications that extend far beyond trade policy.

Experts Predict AI Will Boost U.S. Economic Growth to 2.5% Despite Job Market Fears

Title: Forecasting the Economic Effects of AI | Authors: Ezra Karger, Otto Kuusela, Jason Abaluck, and 14 co-authors

In the largest expert survey on AI's economic impact, researchers polled economists, AI company employees, and forecasters about the technology's effects through 2030. Despite expectations of major AI advances, experts predict only modest labor market disruptions but significant productivity gains that could push GDP growth to 2.5%—well above typical government projections. The consensus view suggests AI's economic transformation may be more gradual and beneficial than popular narratives suggest, though the range of expert opinions reveals substantial uncertainty about this defining economic shift.

Macroeconomics

This week's macroeconomic research spans from AI's economic potential to the mechanics of financial bubbles, offering fresh insights into some of today's most pressing economic questions.

Forecasting the Economic Effects of AI

Authors: Ezra Karger, Otto Kuusela, Jason Abaluck, Kevin A. Bryan, Basil Halperin, Todd R. Jones, Connacher Murphy, Philip Trammell, Matt Reynolds, Dan Mayland, Ria Viswanathan, Ananaya Mittal, Rebecca Ceppas de Castro, Josh Rosenberg, Philip Tetlock

This comprehensive survey of economists, AI company employees, policy researchers, forecasters, and the general public reveals surprising consensus about AI's economic impact. All groups expect substantial AI advances by 2030 and anticipate annual GDP growth of 2.5%—significantly higher than typical government and private forecasts of 1.7-2.0%. Even in rapid AI progress scenarios where machines surpass human performance on many tasks, experts predict substantial but not historically unprecedented economic changes. Why it matters: These findings suggest AI optimism may be more widespread than previously thought, with important implications for economic planning and policy preparation.

A Model of Leveraged Bubbles

Authors: Nina Biljanovska, Jordi Galí, Lucyna Górnicka, Alexandros P. Vardoulakis

The authors develop a sophisticated model explaining why recessions following asset bubbles with high credit growth are particularly severe and long-lasting. Their framework shows how bubbles initially relax borrowing constraints by inflating collateral values, but create devastating feedback loops when they burst and borrowing limits suddenly tighten. The research identifies optimal policy responses, including borrowing taxes that can substantially reduce both the frequency and severity of bubble-induced recessions. Why it matters: This work provides crucial insights for policymakers seeking to prevent the kind of leveraged bubble cycles that devastated economies in 2008 and could emerge again in today's credit markets.

Trade in AI-Related Products

Authors: Michael E. Waugh

Using innovative large language model classification techniques, this study reveals that AI-related products now account for 23% of U.S. imports in 2025, with imports growing 73% since 2023 compared to just 3% for non-AI products. Mexico and Taiwan emerge as key trading partners, together accounting for about half of all U.S. AI-related trade, while trade policy has generally treated these products lightly with strategic exemptions. Why it matters: These findings highlight how AI is rapidly reshaping global trade patterns and supply chains, with significant implications for trade policy and economic competitiveness.

The Macroeconomic Effects of Bank Regulation

Authors: Thomas Drechsel, Ko Miura

This innovative study uses high-frequency market reactions to Federal Reserve speeches to identify how bank regulation affects the broader economy. The researchers find that tighter regulation reduces banking sector risk but constrains economic activity by increasing banks' funding costs and tightening loan supply—a 10 basis point regulation-induced reduction in bank risk premiums correlates with measurable economic slowdown. Why it matters: This research provides crucial empirical evidence for policymakers navigating the fundamental tradeoff between financial stability and economic growth.

Microeconomics

This week brings fascinating insights into how algorithms enable collusion, strategic pricing in healthcare, and the hidden redistributive effects of payment systems.

Collusion with Optimal Information Disclosure

Authors: Takuo Sugaya, Alexander Wolitzky

As pricing algorithms become ubiquitous, this research reveals how third-party intermediaries can facilitate collusion by strategically sharing market information with competing firms. The study shows that algorithms optimally use "upper censorship"—revealing information only when demand is high or costs are low—leading to rigid prices that can exceed even monopoly levels. Why it matters: This provides crucial evidence for antitrust regulators grappling with algorithmic pricing in an increasingly digital economy.

Profit Regulation and Strategic Transfer Pricing by Vertically Integrated Firms: Evidence from Health Care

Authors: Pragya Kakani, Eric Yde, Genevieve P. Kanter, Richard G. Frank, Amelia M. Bond

When Medicare capped insurer profits, vertically integrated health insurers found a clever workaround: they raised prices at their own pharmacies by 9.5%, effectively "tunneling" profits from the regulated insurance business to their unregulated pharmacy subsidiaries. The federal government ended up paying over one-fifth of these inflated costs. Why it matters: This demonstrates how firms can exploit regulatory gaps through vertical integration, undermining policy effectiveness and increasing healthcare spending.

Who Pays for Payments?

Authors: Mark L. Egan, Gregor Matvos, Amit Seru, Lulu Wang, Vincent Yao

Credit card rewards programs create a hidden redistribution system where cash and debit card users effectively subsidize wealthy credit card users through higher merchant prices. The research reveals that this cross-subsidization depends critically on where different consumers shop—challenging the standard assumption that all payment method users face the same prices at the same merchants. Why it matters: This exposes how payment systems can exacerbate inequality, with lower-income consumers subsidizing rewards for higher-income credit card users.

Zero Energy Day: How Nationwide Blackouts Affect the Economy

Authors: Luis E. Gonzales, Koichiro Ito, Mar Reguant

Using high-frequency transaction data from Chile's nationwide blackout, researchers found that economic activity plummeted 35% on the blackout day, but half of these losses were recovered in subsequent days as businesses and consumers rescheduled activities. Why it matters: As power grids face increasing stress from renewable integration and extreme weather, this research provides crucial estimates of blackout costs and highlights the importance of considering economic recovery when valuing grid reliability.

Econometrics & Methods

This week brings exciting developments in measurement and methodology, from revolutionizing advertising incrementality testing to tracking the spread of rigorous empirical methods across economics fields.

Predicted Incrementality by Experimentation (PIE) for Ad Measurement

Authors: Brett R. Gordon, Robert Moakler, Florian Zettelmeyer

The authors tackle a fundamental challenge in digital advertising: while randomized controlled trials provide the best estimates of ad effectiveness, they're expensive and hard to scale. Their solution, PIE, uses machine learning to predict campaign incrementality by training on a sample of RCTs and then applying those learnings to non-experimental campaigns. The key innovation is incorporating post-campaign metrics like exposure rates and conversion data that reflect actual consumer behavior. Why it matters: This could dramatically reduce the cost of measuring advertising effectiveness while maintaining scientific rigor, potentially transforming how billions in ad spend are evaluated.

Tracking the Credibility Revolution across Fields

Authors: Paul Goldsmith-Pinkham

Using text analysis of over 44,000 papers from NBER and top journals, this study maps how experimental and quasi-experimental methods have spread beyond applied microeconomics. While 63% of applied micro papers now use credibility revolution methods, finance lags at 47% and macro/other fields at just 39%. Finance and macro are roughly where applied micro was in 2008-2010, suggesting a significant but delayed adoption pattern. Why it matters: This provides concrete evidence of economics' methodological evolution and highlights persistent gaps in rigorous empirical practice across subfields.

Disagreement of Disagreement

Authors: Christian L. Goulding, Campbell R. Harvey, Hrvoje Kurtović

The researchers discover that major proxies for investor disagreement have nearly zero correlation with each other, creating a fundamental measurement problem in asset pricing research. They develop a new theoretical model and disagreement measure (DIS) that better predicts returns across different market conditions and provides fresh insights into the idiosyncratic volatility puzzle. The work suggests that multiple channels of disagreement operate simultaneously in markets. Why it matters: This resolves longstanding puzzles in finance while providing practitioners and researchers with more reliable tools for measuring market sentiment and pricing anomalies.

Wellbeing, Youth and Survey Mode: Comparisons across 23 Countries Using Three International Datasets

Authors: David G. Blanchflower, Alex Bryson

This methodological investigation reveals that survey mode dramatically affects conclusions about youth wellbeing trends. Comparing face-to-face, telephone, and web-based surveys across 23 countries, the authors find that evidence for declining youth wellbeing is strongest in web-based surveys but much weaker in telephone surveys. The age-wellbeing relationship varies systematically by survey methodology, raising questions about which findings reflect true population trends versus measurement artifacts. Why it matters: As policymakers increasingly rely on wellbeing data to guide interventions, understanding how survey design affects results is crucial for accurate measurement and effective policy responses.
